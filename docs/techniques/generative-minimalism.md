# Generative Minimalism

The aesthetic philosophy behind effective plotter art. Less is more — constraint as creative force.

## Core Principles

### 1. Constraint as Creative Force
Plotter art operates under natural constraints that become strengths:
- **Single line width**: Forces tonal solutions through density and direction
- **No fills**: Shading must emerge from line arrangement
- **Single color** (usually): All variation comes from line behavior
- **Physical medium**: Paper, ink, and pen characteristics ground the digital

### 2. Mathematics as Aesthetic
Equations generate form directly. The beauty is in the mathematical relationship, not in decorating it:
- Periodicity and symmetry from trigonometric functions
- Self-similarity from recursion
- Emergence from iterated simple rules
- Phase space exploration through parameter variation

### 3. Controlled Randomness
Pure randomness is noise. Pure order is mechanical. The art is in the balance:
- **Perlin noise > uniform random**: Coherent variation, not chaos
- **Seeded randomness**: Reproducible, editable variation
- **Organic perturbation on geometric scaffolding**: The scaffold provides structure; the noise provides life

## Visual Vocabulary

### Line Density as Value
The only way a pen creates "tone":
- Dense lines = dark
- Sparse lines = light
- Gradient spacing = smooth transition
- Abrupt spacing change = hard edge

### Implied Depth
Without color or shading:
- Line compression = receding surface (foreshortening)
- Consistent stroke weight = flat plane
- Radial patterns = spherical forms
- Parallel convergence = perspective depth

### Negative Space
What you don't draw matters as much as what you draw:
- Empty areas pull the eye
- Strategic void creates breathing room
- Silhouette formed by hatching termination

## Composition Patterns

### The Warped Grid
Regular grid + single warp function = immediate visual interest. The grid provides context, the warp provides content.

### The Flow Field
Particles following a vector field. Beauty emerges from the interaction of many simple paths following the same rules.

### The Recursive Subdivision
Divide space according to a rule. Repeat. The rule's simplicity vs. the output's complexity creates the tension.

### The Modulated Parameter
One variable changes across space. Everything else stays constant. The eye reads the modulation as meaning.

## Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

### Over-Decoration
Adding detail for its own sake. If every surface is maximally hatched, nothing stands out.

### Competing Effects
Multiple strong visual techniques fighting for attention. One dominant technique per composition, supported by simpler elements.

### Over-Parameterization
Too many controls = no coherent vision. The best compositions have 2-3 meaningful parameters.

### Complexity Without Structure
Random scatter of elements without visual hierarchy. The eye needs an entry point and a journey.

## Referenced Artists
- **Bridget Riley**: Maximum effect from minimum means. Precise repetition creates vibration.
- **Victor Vasarely**: Geometric illusion. Simple shapes, complex perception.
- **Sol LeWitt**: Rule-based wall drawings. The instructions are the art.
- **Vera Molnar**: Pioneer of computer-generated art. Systematic exploration of variation.
- **Manfred Mohr**: Hypercube projections. Mathematical structure as aesthetic content.

## Application to Hatch3d
1. **Resist the urge to fill**: Leave surfaces partially hatched. Let the form suggest itself.
2. **One dominant technique per composition**: Choose hatching OR flow OR stippling. Don't combine everything.
3. **Parameter discipline**: 2-3 meaningful macros per composition. Each should have a clear visual effect.
4. **Mathematical coherence**: Surfaces, hatching, and transforms should share mathematical DNA (same frequencies, related ratios).
5. **Edition thinking**: Design compositions that produce families of related prints through parameter variation, not one-offs.
